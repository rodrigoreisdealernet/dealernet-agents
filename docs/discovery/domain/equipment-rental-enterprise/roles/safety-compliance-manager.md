## Identity & context
This is the cross-branch owner for the safety and DOT-compliance program: usually a regional or
HQ specialist sitting between branch managers and the operations executive. In Dealernet's target
segment, branch leaders still carry day-to-day yard and driver compliance, but this role owns the
enterprise program the branches cannot reliably run alone: incident logging, audit cadence,
training currency, driver-file oversight, and leadership reporting. Sources:
[Sunbelt assistant manager posting](https://careers.sunbeltrentals.com/assistant-manager/job/P1-5980221-0),
[Mazzotta regional director posting](https://www.rermag.com/news-analysis/headline-news/article/55320125/regional-director-of-branch-operations),
[OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/),
[FMCSA HOS summary](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/regulations/hours-service/summary-hours-service-regulations).

The org shape is still a draft to validate with SMEs. In some businesses this is a dedicated
Safety & Compliance Manager; in others it is an EHS or regional operations leader with a strong
specialist coordinator underneath. What is clear from the evidence is that the work exists and
expands with branch count, CDL driver count, and mixed-fleet complexity.

## Goals & motivations
Their goal is simple to say and hard to deliver: keep people safe, keep the company audit-ready,
and keep compliance drift from becoming an injury, a citation, or a branch shutdown. They are
measured less by visible heroics than by the absence of bad surprises: current certifications,
closed corrective actions, complete driver files, timely OSHA reporting, and credible safety
trends for leadership. Sources:
[OSHA powered industrial trucks](https://www.osha.gov/powered-industrial-trucks),
[OSHA cranes and derricks](https://www.osha.gov/cranes-derricks),
[OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/),
[EquipmentShare safety](https://equipmentshare.com/safety).

Motivation comes from risk containment and consistency. They do not want one branch running a
tight program while another quietly accumulates expired certifications, overdue corrective actions,
or HOS violations that only surface during an incident or audit.

## A day / week in the life
The daily rhythm starts with exceptions: overnight incidents, near-miss follow-up, expiring
credentials, driver-behavior alerts, and HOS issues that need branch attention before trucks or
operators go back out. Sources:
[FMCSA HOS summary](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/regulations/hours-service/summary-hours-service-regulations),
[EquipmentShare safety](https://equipmentshare.com/safety).

The weekly cadence is program maintenance: reviewing the OSHA log, checking training and operator
currency, and making sure branches are closing prior findings instead of carrying them forward.
Monthly work turns into rollups and follow-through: audit schedules, leadership KPI packs, and
the uncomfortable branch conversations when a recurring gap is no longer a local exception. Source:
[OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/).

## Frustrations & pains
The biggest pain is fragmented evidence. Incident facts, driver logs, audit findings, training
records, and equipment history live in different systems, but the deadlines do not wait for the
paperwork to catch up. Sources:
[OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/),
[EquipmentShare safety](https://equipmentshare.com/safety).

The second pain is distributed ownership. Branch managers are expected to maintain DOT and safety
compliance while also running rental, yard, and dispatch operations, which means the enterprise
program can drift until a specialist notices the pattern. Sources:
[Sunbelt assistant manager posting](https://careers.sunbeltrentals.com/assistant-manager/job/P1-5980221-0),
[Mazzotta regional director posting](https://www.rermag.com/news-analysis/headline-news/article/55320125/regional-director-of-branch-operations).

The third pain is timing pressure. Recordability and reportability decisions, post-accident
testing, and expiring certifications all create fast clocks with real regulatory and operational
consequences if the wrong call is made or made too late. Sources:
[OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/),
[FMCSA regulations index](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/regulations/title49/part/382).

## Tools today
This role lives in a stitched-together admin stack: OSHA forms and portals, FMCSA/ELD data,
driver qualification files, an LMS or certification tracker, audit checklists, spreadsheets, and
the core rental/HR systems that provide equipment and employee context. Sources:
[OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/),
[FMCSA HOS summary](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/regulations/hours-service/summary-hours-service-regulations),
[EquipmentShare safety](https://equipmentshare.com/safety).

Even in a mature operation, the work still involves a lot of swivel-chair coordination between
branch leaders, service, logistics, HR, and leadership reporting. The specialist role exists
because no single branch system cleanly owns all of that.

## Decisions they own
They decide what is recordable, reportable, overdue, disqualifying, or severe enough to escalate.
That includes whether an injury belongs on the OSHA log, whether a driver or operator can keep
working, whether a finding is a local correction or a multi-branch program failure, and which
trends leadership needs to act on now. Sources:
[OSHA powered industrial trucks](https://www.osha.gov/powered-industrial-trucks),
[OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/),
[FMCSA HOS summary](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/regulations/hours-service/summary-hours-service-regulations).

That judgment profile is why most tasks below are `assist`, not `automate`: the system can watch
deadlines, assemble evidence, and draft rollups, but decisions that affect employability,
regulatory posture, or branch operations still need human disposition.

## What "amazing" would do for them
Amazing would turn compliance from detective work into supervised operations. Expiring
certifications, driver-file gaps, incident deadlines, and overdue corrective actions would surface
as one scoped queue with the supporting facts already attached. The system would draft the monthly
safety pack automatically and flag which branches genuinely need attention, instead of forcing this
role to spend reporting time finding data that already exists somewhere else.

### Notable pains & agentic opportunities
- **Compliance deadline sentinel** — strongest `assist` opportunity. Watch OSHA-reporting clocks,
  certification expiries, HOS exceptions, and audit-due dates in one queue with the affected
  branch, employee, and equipment already scoped. Evidence:
  [OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/),
  [FMCSA HOS summary](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/regulations/hours-service/summary-hours-service-regulations),
  [EquipmentShare safety](https://equipmentshare.com/safety).
- **Audit and corrective-action prep** — strong `assist` opportunity. Pre-build branch audit
  agendas from prior findings, open actions, and recent incidents so the manager spends time on the
  judgment, not the assembly. Evidence:
  [EquipmentShare safety](https://equipmentshare.com/safety),
  [Mazzotta regional director posting](https://www.rermag.com/news-analysis/headline-news/article/55320125/regional-director-of-branch-operations).
- **Safety KPI pack synthesis** — clearest `automate` candidate. Assemble recurring monthly
  leadership reporting from incident, audit, training, and driver-behavior datasets without making
  the regulatory judgment calls for the human. Evidence:
  [EquipmentShare safety](https://equipmentshare.com/safety),
  [OSHA recordkeeping](https://www.osha.gov/recordkeeping/).
