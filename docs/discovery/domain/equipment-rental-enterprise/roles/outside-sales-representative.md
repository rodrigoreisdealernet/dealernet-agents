## Identity & context
The Outside Sales Representative / Territory Account Manager is the field-facing owner of
contractor relationships. Unlike the counter role, which handles inbound transactional demand,
this role works a territory: active jobsites, customer offices, estimators, and procurement
teams. United Rentals describes the track explicitly as **field-based** and separate from inside
sales, with roles spanning contractor supplies and national accounts. Sources:
[United Rentals sales careers](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-areas/sales),
[RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service).

In Wynne's enterprise multi-branch contractor/project segment, this role is the downstream
recipient of the branch handoff when an opportunity stops being a simple counter order and
becomes account development, project pursuit, or negotiated relationship work. They are
customer-facing, mobile, and commercially accountable, but still depend on branch operations for
real availability and execution capacity. Sources:
[United Rentals sales careers](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-areas/sales),
[United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
[RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service).

## Goals & motivations
The role is trying to grow revenue without degrading trust: win the next phase of the project,
recover dormant accounts, defend key customers from competitors, and bring the branch deals it
can actually execute. United Rentals frames the development path around territory coverage, CRM
use, active-customer growth, and rental revenue growth, which makes the commercial objective
explicit. Sources: [United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
[United Rentals sales careers](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-areas/sales).

Motivation is strongly relationship-driven. RER's guidance is blunt that some opportunities should
move from the counter to an outside salesperson because that person is better positioned to
develop and maintain the relationship over time. In practice that means this role is measured not
just on closed rentals, but on being top-of-mind when the contractor starts the next job or hits
an exception on the current one. Sources:
[RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service),
[Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/).

## A day / week in the life
The week is split between the field and the follow-up loop it creates. Mornings often start in
CRM and account history: which customers are active, which jobs are slipping, which proposals need
revision, which dormant accounts are worth another pass. From there the role is on the road —
visiting jobsites, checking in with project contacts, probing for the next phase of work, and
capturing notes before the context disappears. Sources:
[United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
[Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/).

The slower rhythm is proposal and relationship management. Large opportunities require negotiated
quotes, internal coordination with the branch, and repeated follow-up. Even after equipment is
delivered, the work continues through satisfaction checks, expansion conversations, and win-back
campaigns for accounts that have gone quiet. Sources:
[RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service),
[Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/).

## Frustrations & pains
The biggest pain is fragmented account memory. The role is expected to remember purchasing
history, past conversations, open opportunities, and the customer's likely next need, yet that
knowledge is often scattered across CRM entries, account history, phones, and personal notes. RER
explicitly calls out the need to use a customer database and preserve notes about past
conversations and facts about the business. Sources:
[RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service),
[Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/).

The second pain is staying proactive at scale. The industry guidance says not to wait for
customers to return and to run direct-mail or telephone campaigns to reintroduce capabilities, but
that is easy advice to give and hard to operationalize when the same rep is also visiting jobs,
pricing deals, and chasing execution issues. Sources:
[RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service),
[United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development).

The third pain is selling with imperfect operational truth. The rep can build demand and negotiate
terms, but still needs reliable answers from branch operations on availability, delivery capacity,
and what the business can safely promise. That makes the role a natural `assist` surface rather
than an `automate` surface for most meaningful work. Sources:
[United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
[Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/).

## Tools today
The core toolset is a CRM plus the rental system of record. United Rentals trains sales reps on
CRM tools, quotes, rental, sales, reservations, and territory management, while Texada positions
mobile CRM, lead management, quote generation, and customer-lifecycle visibility as the daily
surface for equipment sales teams. Sources:
[United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
[Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/).

In the Wynne segment, that means the rep lives across phone, email, mobile CRM, account history,
RentalMan quoting and pricing context, and the branch's informal communication loops. The hard
part is not just having the data; it is getting the right account context and branch reality in
one place before the rep talks to the customer. Sources:
[Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/),
[Wynne RentalMan](https://www.wynnesystems.com/solutions/rentalman/).

## Decisions they own
This role decides where to spend field time, which contractors deserve active pursuit, whether a
request should become a negotiated proposal, how aggressively to price a deal, which dormant
accounts are genuinely recoverable, and when a customer issue needs retention work before spend
moves elsewhere. Sources:
[United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
[RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service),
[United Rentals sales careers](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-areas/sales).

That judgment mix explains why the task table is all `assist`. The work is rich in research,
prioritization, and drafting, but almost every meaningful output is customer-facing,
money-adjacent, or both, so the rep still owns the disposition.

## What "amazing" would do for them
Amazing would make the rep walk into every call or site visit with the account already
reconstructed: recent rentals, open quotes, branch risks, dormant opportunities worth reviving,
and the most likely next question the customer will ask. It would not auto-negotiate or auto-send
commercial commitments, but it would cut the time spent rebuilding context, chasing internal
answers, and remembering which relationship needs attention first.

### Notable pains & agentic opportunities
- **Territory account brief** — strongest `assist` opportunity. Before a customer call or site
  visit, assemble the live account picture: recent rentals, upcoming project signals, dormant
  opportunities, promised follow-ups, and branch-side execution risks, so the rep disposes from a
  prepared brief instead of reconstructing context manually. Evidence:
  [United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
  [Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/),
  [RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service).
- **Dormant-account win-back queue** — another strong `assist` candidate. Rank which lapsed
  accounts are most worth pursuing now, draft the re-engagement angle from history and product
  fit, and leave the outreach itself with the rep. Evidence:
  [United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
  [RER customer-service guidance](https://www.rermag.com/mag/article/20930805/14-tips-to-legendary-customer-service).
- **Proposal prep co-pilot** — bounded but still `assist`. Prepare first-pass project proposals
  from account history, likely fleet mix, and branch assumptions, while keeping all
  customer-facing pricing and commitments behind human review. Evidence:
  [United Rentals sales development](https://careers.unitedrentals.com/career-development),
  [Texada sales solutions](https://texadasoftware.com/equipment-growth-platform/sales/).
